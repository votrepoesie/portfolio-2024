Overview ✨

Young adults often grapple with stress, anxiety, and self-critical thinking. These challenges hinder confidence, exacerbate anxiety and depression, and impede personal growth. In this project, we aim to help young adults overcome these challenges by creating an immersive VR experience that fosters self-compassion and emotional resilience, ultimately promoting mental well-being.

Research & Ideation ✨

The Problem

To better understand how young adults feel about processing their emotions and their mental wellbeing, we conducted six focus groups with 2-3 participants each.

We provided a slide deck of 50 metaphors to help them express how they think and feel. Participants chose metaphors they resonated with in advance, then they shared their thoughts with the group.

Overall, we found that young adults need help with managing academic and work-related stress, which make them feel anxious and inadequate.

New Metaphors is a creative toolkit to generate ideas & reframe problems, developed by the Imaginaries Lab @ Eindhoven University of Technology.

Research Findings

Using the entire body to move and process information.

Immersive environments are recognized for their potential in psychotherapy. These environments provide a safe and controlled space for individuals to confront emotional challenges. Research also highlights how VR can create experiences that promote emotional processing and self-awareness, essential components of effective therapy. 

Games create the ideal environment for learning.

Games allow for the formation of a magic circle: a safe, playful space that encourages exploration. Within the magic circle, players can safely make mistakes and act in ways that are not in accordance with their everyday persona, thereby allowing the game to become an ideal environment for learning.

Nudging the player to be open and honest about their feelings.

We conducted focus groups to understand challenges that young adults face with managing mental wellbeing. We decided to employ cognitive distancing to encourage players to be more open and honest about their feelings.

Design Opportunities

We explored game ideas that used bodily movements to combat negative emotions, inspired by therapeutic techniques including ACT (acceptance & commitment therapy) and EMDR (eye movement desensitization and reprocessing).

The core idea of our design is to allow users to confront their thoughts in a safe environment and be able to let their thoughts go, and we hypothesize that physical movements can help achieve this transformational goal.

Rapid Prototyping ✨

We wanted the game to be lighthearted and fun, so players don’t feel burdened by confronting their negative emotions. We want players to leave the game feeling refreshed and confident.

Thus, we created a narrative to distance players from real life, and we reframed the narrative from helping yourself to learning how to help yourself from helping others.

The game takes place in a beautiful but troubled village, and players follow a storyline where they help the villagers to destroy negative thoughts. Through helping villagers, they engage with tasks to help ground them and relieve stress through bodily movements.

Ideating the core narrative and points of interaction

At this point we ask ourselves: is there a way to quickly test and iterate ideas without actually building them in VR?

As a result, we used Wizard of Oz technique to conduct two rounds of user testing, hence, a lot of arts and crafts. The final round of testing was done with Meta Horizon World.

Now, let's get into rapid prototyping & testing the interactions!

#1 Collect Positive Thoughts

For the first task, players were instructed to do a power pose then reach out their arms to pick apples, stretching their body in the process. As they pick these apples, they read out the self-affirmative messages floating next to the apples.

To quickly test the effectiveness of this interaction, we used physical props including a Christmas tree and some origami apples.

User Flow

  1. Get ready with a power pose
  2. As you pick apples, read out the messages floating next to them
  3. Reach for the tallest apple
  4. Reach out to pick apples on the furthest left & right at the same time
  5. Pick the one that looks the most delicious

Observation

  1. Stretching helped players feel relaxed
  2. Reading out loud helped players internalize the messages
  3. Players felt more confident after completing the task

After 2 rounds of "wizard of oz" prototypes, we tested the interactions in VR:

Picking apples with positive messages

#2 Help someone else overcome negative thoughts

The player helps a troubled villager by destroying negative thoughts that plague them. We tried different interactions including tracing words, popping balloons, and throwing darts.

User Flow

  1. Players encounter an NPC in distress
  2. Players destroy the NPC's negative thoughts through physical movements
  3. NPC asks for advice on how to better manage their emotions
  4. Player gives spoken or written advice

Observation

  1. Because players helped the NPC and gave advice, they feel more emotionally connected to the NPC
  2. Physical movements were effective in relieving stress
  3. Players gained more perspective by helping the NPC

After 2 rounds of prototyping, we tested the interaction in VR but made adjustments based on what assets were available. We couldn't pop balloons, but we could throw darts!

Throwing darts at negative messages for emotional release

#3 Confront your own negative thought

For the final task, the player confronts their own negative thought and overcomes it. The first two iterations used paper, where the player wrote down what bothered them that day with their non-dominant hand, then ripped / teared / punched through it.

Ripping / tearing / punching through paper, then reveal to an ending scene with confetti

User Flow

  1. Prior to entering the game, players write down a negative thought and put it into a box
  2. NPC prompts players to imagine the solid cube as the box
  3. Players destroy the cube with a pickaxe

Observation

  1. Players enjoy the game more when they are confronted with a personal thought, because it makes destroying the box more cathartic
  2. Players prefer writing the thought in VR for a more consistent experience

Learnings ✨

Prepare for technical and physical difficulties.

We didn't anticipate the technical difficulties and the time it would take for the users to familiarize themselves with the VR controls, which was not a concern in previous physical prototypes. Thus, it is important to reset our expectations of how quickly our users will understand an interaction paradigm they're not accustomed to.

Create personalized experiences to maximize emotional release.

To further personalize the experience, we will integrate elements where players can see what they wrote in VR. This will allow players to reflect on their thoughts and emotions, making the VR experience even more effective in promoting self-awareness and self-compassion.

Expand the storyline and narrative depth for more immersion.

Our game currently consists of only three stages, which we recognize is limiting. To meet our vision, we aim to develop a fully realized virtual world with an intuitive and cohesive story. This will involve streamlining the narrative, reducing the number of characters, and focusing on a more personalized and engaging storyline. This enhanced narrative depth will draw players deeper into the VR world, making their mental health journey more relatable and impactful.

Reflection ✨

This project was my second VR attempt and the first with HCI considerations, so I was out of my comfort zone a lot, but I learned to make progress in small increments and to be okay with asking for help.

After all, the best way to learn is to do.

Be scrappy. Take risks. Use whatever means to quickly test the idea and be fearless of technological constraints, because we can learn from failures and compromises.
